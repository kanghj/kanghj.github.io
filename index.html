Hong Jin Kang

PhD Student at Singapore Management University

I am a PhD candidate at Singapore Management University under the SOAR group. I am very fortunate to be advised by Prof. David Lo and to collaborate with many brilliant and talented researchers.

Software is eating the world but there is an incoming software apocalypse. We need better ways of understanding and engineering software systems. My research goal is to develop new approaches to detect bugs by studying the principles from different fields to mine abstractions and patterns of programs.

[Resume] [Google Scholar] [DBLP]

Many aspects of Software Engineering interest me, but my primary interest is to find better program abstractions. To do so, I have explored the use of specification mining, test case generation, and vector representations of programs.

Mining specifications. My primary interest is on mining rules and specifications of software. We have studied the use of search-based sofware testing on the inference of automaton models [TOSEM 2020], as well as inferring security policies for an IoT software system [ICST 2021].

At the moment, I am studying other representations of source code, as well as rules in emerging software systems. Better techniques to infer specifications will help in debugging and catching bugs.

Program transformation. We developed Coccinelle4J [ECOOP 2019] for program transformation of Java programs, extending the Coccinelle program matching and transformation tool. Coccinelle is widely adopted in the Linux kernel and C systems software. To build better tools, there are many lessons we can learn from Coccinelle.

Much of our work have been on Android, including inferring rules to migrate usages of deprecated API methods with replacement methods [ICSME 2019, ICPC 2020 ERA]. Better research on program transformation will help automate more programming.

Representation of programs. To better understand software systems, I am also interested in how best to represent a piece of program. There has been growing interest in using techniques from machine learning, e.g. the embedding of entities into a low dimensional vector space.

We have investigated the generalizability of a representative token-based embeddings [ASE 2019], and proposed embeddings for representing code changes [ICSE 2020]. Better representations of programs will enable better machine learning on programs.
